# Assumptions
*AIgnition 3.0 — Probabilistic Revenue Forecasting*

## Data assumptions

- `data/` holds the **real challenge dataset** (`google_ads_campaign_stats.csv`, `bing_campaign_stats.csv`, `meta_ads_campaign_stats.csv` — the actual files from the brief's linked Drive folder), not synthetic samples. See `docs/SCHEMA_ANALYSIS.md` for the full column-by-column breakdown.
- Channel is inferred from the CSV filename by keyword match (`google`, `meta`/`facebook`, `bing`/`microsoft`/`ms`), not a hardcoded filename — Bing's real file is named `bing_campaign_stats.csv` with no `ms_ads_` prefix, so this matters in practice, not just in theory.
- **Each of the three platforms exports a genuinely different schema**, not variations on one naming convention — `generate_features.py` detects which by a distinctive marker column (`segments_date` for Google, `TimePeriod` for Bing, `date_start` for Meta) and maps each explicitly, including unit conversions (Google's `metrics_cost_micros` needs ÷1,000,000) and one field that means something different than its name suggests (Meta's `conversion` column is conversion *value*/revenue, not a count — verified: fractional currency amounts frequently exceeding click count, and no count column exists in that export at all). A generic alias-table fallback still exists for any file that doesn't match one of these three (e.g. our own earlier synthetic samples), but the real dataset needed explicit per-platform handling, not a shared alias table.
- Real platform "campaign type" enums (`PERFORMANCE_MAX`, `PerformanceMax`, `Audience`, `VIDEO`, `DEMAND_GEN`, ...) are canonicalized to our `shopping/brand/search/retargeting/display/other` vocabulary via a case/whitespace/underscore-insensitive lookup. Google/Bing's `SEARCH`/`Search` is further split into `brand` vs `search` using the `_TM_` (trademark/brand-term) vs `_NTM_` (non-trademark) naming convention already present in the real campaign names — verified `_tm_` doesn't falsely substring-match inside `_ntm_`.
- If a file has no `campaign_type` column at all (Meta), it's inferred from keywords in `campaign_name` (`remarketing`/`retarget`→retargeting, `prospecting`→display, `shopping`/`pmax`→shopping, `brand`→brand, `search`→search, `display`→display, `generic`→search as a final fallback before `other`), checked in that priority order so more specific intent signals win over generic ones.
- All monetary values are USD. Revenue is last-click attribution as provided by each platform (per the brief: "existing attribution should be treated as the source of truth" — no custom attribution modeling).
- Missing dates within a campaign's date range are filled with zero spend/revenue/impressions/clicks/conversions, not dropped.
- Rows with negative spend are dropped as data-entry errors (logged to stderr, not silently discarded) — none were found in the real dataset; this guard is defensive for whatever the held-out test set contains.
- **`campaign_name` is NOT globally unique** — 27 real campaign names (e.g. `Pmax_NTM_Campaign_01` through `_19`) exist as unrelated campaigns in both the Google and Bing exports. Consistency validation and every model dictionary in `ForecastingTribunal` are keyed by `(channel, campaign_name)`, not `campaign_name` alone.

## Model assumptions

- **Aggregate-period, not daily, forecasting** (per the brief's explicit constraint): XGBoost and Ridge predict one representative future daily revenue rate from the latest known feature row and scale it by the period length (30/60/90 days) — they do not iterate day-by-day. Prophet forecasts daily internally (its actual strength) and sums those days per period, which is more accurate but doesn't contradict the "aggregate-period" framing since the output is still one number per period.
- **Prophet is skipped below 60 days of campaign history** (47 of 136 real campaigns) and relies on XGBoost + Ridge alone (with ensemble weights renormalized across the remaining two).
- **Prophet's yearly seasonality is conditional on history length**, not always on: full `yearly_seasonality=True` requires ≥730 days of history, `'auto'` for ≥365 days, and it's disabled entirely below a year. This was not a planned simplification — it was found by testing: forcing `yearly_seasonality=True` on a 90-day series made Prophet extrapolate wildly past the training window (day-30 forecast of +$4k/day swinging to day-60 at -$90k/day for the same campaign), because fitting annual Fourier terms with under a year of evidence has nothing real to anchor on. The real dataset spans over two years for most campaigns, so this mostly resolves to full yearly seasonality in practice.
- **XGBoost quantile regression, not bootstrap ensembling.** A 100-model-per-campaign bootstrap (each a full 200-tree model) was implemented first and worked, but pickled to ~190MB for just 7 sample campaigns — that fails GitHub's 100MB file limit, and scaled to the real dataset's 136 campaigns would have been far worse. Switched to `xgboost`'s native `reg:quantileerror` objective (xgboost≥2.0), which predicts P10/P50/P90 directly from one model per campaign. `random_state=42` throughout, consistent with the "seed everything" rule, instead of varying 0-99 across bootstrap resamples.
- **The trained pickle is gzip-compressed transparently** (`ForecastingTribunal.save`/`load` wrap `gzip.open`, same file path). 136 real campaigns still pickled to ~108MB raw even with the single-model-per-campaign approach above — gzip cuts that to ~14MB with zero model-quality tradeoff (same `n_estimators`), leaving comfortable margin under GitHub's 100MB limit.
- **Ensemble weights are data-driven, tuned against a rolling-origin backtest, not just domain intuition.** The original weights (Prophet 0.5 for Shopping/Brand, the dataset's dominant campaign type) were replaced after `src/backtest.py --cutoffs 4` measured Prophet as consistently ~1.85x worse than XGBoost/Ridge (pooled MAE $7,977 vs $4,258/$4,318) at every single cutoff - not noise. Cutting Prophet's weight (not dropping it - that measured worse median error) improved pooled MAE 20.0% over baseline (up from 13.5%) and closed most of a real interval-calibration gap (P10-P90 coverage 68.7% → 81.6%, against an 80% target, via `INTERVAL_WIDEN_FACTOR=1.5`). Full before/after numbers: `docs/TECHNICAL_DOC.md`.
- **Ensemble weights fall back to the Display/Other bucket** for any `campaign_type` value not in the fixed weight table, rather than erroring.
- **Disagreement score uses only the models that actually ran** for a campaign (e.g. just XGBoost + Ridge if Prophet was skipped for a short series) — not a fixed three-model formula.
- **Projected spend for ROAS** is the trailing 28-day average daily spend × period length, unless a caller passes `future_spend_overrides` to `ForecastingTribunal.predict()` (keyed by `(channel, campaign_name)`) — which the UI's budget simulator effectively does at a higher level, see below. This is a "continue current spend rate" baseline scenario, not a spend forecast in its own right.
- **Campaigns present at predict time but absent from the trained pickle get a naive fallback prediction** (trailing 28-day average daily revenue × period, ±30% band, `uncertainty_level` fixed to `HIGH`, a warning logged to stderr) rather than silently vanishing from `predictions.csv`. The submission guide states the pickle is never retrained at test time, so if the held-out data includes a genuinely new campaign, this is the safety net.
- **On the real dataset, uncertainty skews HIGH** (300 of 408 output rows) — this is not a bug: it's concentrated in data-rich Shopping/PMax campaigns where Prophet, XGBoost, and Ridge genuinely diverge on volatile real daily spend/revenue patterns, and it's *lower* on sparse campaigns (where limited data makes all models converge toward a similar simple estimate). Verified this isn't an artifact of the fallback path or a degenerate model fit — see `docs/SCHEMA_ANALYSIS.md`.

## Business / product assumptions

- **`predictions.csv` has no explicit spend/budget column.** It's algebraically recoverable as `revenue_p50 / roas_p50` (since ROAS is defined as revenue/spend), which the UI uses as each campaign's "current" budget shown pre-filled in editable inputs. (The real exports do carry budget-like columns — `campaign_budget_amount`, `DailyBudget`, `daily_budget` — not currently plumbed through; a possible enhancement, not required for the pipeline to be correct.)
- **The UI's budget simulator holds ROAS constant and scales revenue linearly with the budget input** (`revenue = budget × ROAS`). It does not model diminishing returns from heavier spend — a full media-mix model is explicitly out of scope per the brief ("building a custom attribution engine or a full-scale MMM is outside the scope of this challenge"). This is the same simplification most agency planning spreadsheets already make.
- **Battle View compares allocations at the P50 (expected) level only** — it doesn't have its own P10/P90 toggle; it defers to being a decision-support tool for the "expected case" plan, with the global RiskDial covering the worst/best-case framing elsewhere.
- **The disagreement narrative's "historical ROAS" input is the forecast's own blended P50 ROAS**, not a separate trailing-30-day actuals figure — `predictions.csv` only carries forecasts, not historical actuals, so there's nothing else to pass. This is disclosed to the LLM only as "historical blended ROAS" context for its explanation, not presented to the user as a distinct historical metric anywhere in the UI.
- **LLM calls are on-demand (button click), never auto-fired** on page load or on every RiskDial/period toggle — both to keep the UI responsive and to stay within Groq's free-tier rate limits during a live demo.
- **`frontend/.env` (with the real API key) is gitignored**; only `frontend/.env.example` is committed. Anyone cloning the repo needs their own free Groq key for the UI's AI features — the offline pipeline (`run.sh`) needs no key at all.

## Known limitations

- The API key is exposed in the browser bundle (`VITE_`-prefixed env vars are inlined at build time) — acceptable for a judged hackathon prototype calling a free-tier API directly from the client, not for a production multi-tenant deployment (that would need a thin backend proxy holding the key server-side).
- Campaign-type buckets themselves (which weight set applies to which `campaign_type`) are still domain intuition, not tuned per-bucket - only the weight *values* were tuned against backtest evidence, and that evidence was pooled across all campaign types together (109-162 campaign-forecasts per model), not broken out per bucket, since the dataset doesn't have enough campaigns per (campaign_type, cutoff) combination to tune each bucket independently with any confidence.
- The naive fallback for unseen campaigns (see above) is intentionally simple — a trailing average, not a model — since there's no training data to fit one to at predict time.
- Training on the full real dataset (27,166 feature rows, 136 campaigns) takes roughly a minute on a modern laptop; this is a one-time/occasional cost (`train.py` is never called by `run.sh`), but worth knowing before assuming a re-train is instant.
- **A rolling-origin backtest (`src/backtest.py --cutoffs 4`, see `docs/TECHNICAL_DOC.md`) found and fixed a real problem** (Prophet overweighted relative to its actual accuracy) and measured a genuine 20.0% MAE improvement over a naive baseline plus much-improved interval calibration (68.7% → 81.6% coverage). What's still honestly unresolved: **median absolute error remains slightly worse than the naive baseline** ($998 vs $816 pooled) — for a "typical" campaign, assuming revenue continues at its recent rate is still a marginally better bet than the ensemble's point forecast, even after tuning. Median absolute percentage error also got worse (40.2% → 50.4%) even as dollar-denominated errors improved, which wasn't further investigated given time constraints. None of this means the pipeline is broken — every internal-consistency check still passes — it means the accuracy story is genuinely mixed, not a clean win, and is reported that way rather than only reporting the flattering numbers. There is no realistic path to anything like "95% accuracy" at 30-day campaign-level revenue granularity on this kind of data — daily ad revenue is inherently volatile, and a claim that high would itself be the red flag, not the model.
